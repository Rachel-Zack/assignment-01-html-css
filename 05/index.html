John F. Kennedy

Rachel Zack

John F. Kennedy (1917-1963) was the President of the United States from 1961 to 1963.

Born in 1917 in Boston, Massachusetts into a wealthy, politically prominent family, Kennedy attended private school in New England, then Harvard University, where he graduated in 1940 with a B.A. in International Affairs. He then joined the United States Navy, serving in the Pacific theater during the Second World War. While stationed there, Kennedy's PT boat was sunk by a Japanese destroyer, and he was awarded a Purple Heart for his heroics in rescuing his crew.

Kennedy was elected to the United States House of Representatives in 1946 and the United States Senate in 1952. In 1960, Kennedy ran for President of the United States as a Democrat, defeating Republican Richard Nixon in a close election.

As President, Kennedy dealt with communist influence in Germany, Cuba and Southeast Asia while also addressing racial injustice in the American South. Kennedy's record was mixed: at home, he was slow on civil rights; abroad, he had successes like the Partial Test Ban Treaty alongside failures like the Bay of Pigs. However, the handsome and charismatic Kennedy was well-liked by Americans and the world.

Kennedy was assassinated in Dallas, Texas, on November 22, 1963. His death was a traumatic experience for the American people, and has been referred to by both contemporaneous commentators and historians as a "loss of innocence" in the postwar United States.
